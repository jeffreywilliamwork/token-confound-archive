To prove that the fundamental group of the circle \( \pi_1(S^1) \) is isomorphic to the integers \( \mathbb{Z} \), follow these steps:

### 1. **Construct the Universal Covering Space**
The universal cover of \( S^1 \) is \( \mathbb{R} \), with the covering map \( p: \mathbb{R} \to S^1 \) defined by \( p(t) = e^{2\pi i t} \). Since \( \mathbb{R} \) is simply connected, it is the universal cover.

### 2. **Define the Lifting Correspondence**
Fix base points \( x_0 = 1 \in S^1 \) and \( \tilde{x}_0 = 0 \in \mathbb{R} \). For a loop \( \gamma: [0,1] \to S^1 \) based at \( x_0 \), the unique lift \( \tilde{\gamma}: [0,1] \to \mathbb{R} \) starting at \( 0 \) ends at an integer \( n \in \mathbb{Z} \). The **lifting correspondence** maps \( [\gamma] \in \pi_1(S^1) \) to \( n \in \mathbb{Z} \).

### 3. **Show the Correspondence is a Homomorphism**
Let \( \gamma, \delta \) be loops in \( S^1 \), with lifts \( \tilde{\gamma} \) ending at \( n \) and \( \tilde{\delta} \) ending at \( m \). The concatenation \( \gamma * \delta \) lifts to a path starting at \( 0 \), following \( \tilde{\gamma} \) to \( n \), then following \( \tilde{\delta} \) translated by \( n \) (since \( p(n + t) = p(t) \)). The endpoint is \( n + m \), so the correspondence maps \( [\gamma] * [\delta] \) to \( n + m \), preserving group structure.

### 4. **Prove Injectivity via Homotopy Lifting**
Suppose two loops \( \gamma, \delta \) have lifts ending at the same integer \( n \). By the homotopy lifting property, any homotopy between \( \gamma \) and \( \delta \) lifts to a homotopy between their lifts in \( \mathbb{R} \). Since \( \mathbb{R} \) is contractible, all paths with the same endpoints are homotopic, so \( \gamma \) and \( \delta \) are homotopic in \( S^1 \). Thus, the kernel is trivial, proving injectivity.

### 5. **Prove Surjectivity with Explicit Loops**
For any \( n \in \mathbb{Z} \), define the loop \( \omega_n(t) = e^{2\pi i n t} \). Its lift \( \tilde{\omega}_n(t) = nt \) starts at \( 0 \) and ends at \( n \). Thus, \( \omega_n \) is mapped to \( n \), showing surjectivity.

### Conclusion
The lifting correspondence \( \pi_1(S^1) \to \mathbb{Z} \) is a bijective homomorphism, hence an isomorphism. Therefore, \( \pi_1(S^1) \cong \mathbb{Z} \).
