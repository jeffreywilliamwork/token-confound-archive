To determine why comparison-based sorting algorithms cannot surpass O(n log n) time complexity in the worst case, we analyze the problem through the decision tree model, derive an information-theoretic lower bound, and demonstrate that mergesort achieves this bound.

---

### **1. Decision Tree Model**
Comparison-based sorting algorithms determine the order of elements via pairwise comparisons. Each comparison splits the set of possible permutations into subsets. This process is modeled as a **decision tree**:
- **Internal nodes**: Represent comparisons (e.g., "Is \(a_i < a_j\)?").
- **Leaves**: Represent permutations of the input (i.e., sorted orders).
- **Path from root to leaf**: Represents the sequence of comparisons to reach a sorted order.

For \(n\) elements, there are \(n!\) permutations. A valid decision tree must have at least \(n!\) leaves.

---

### **2. Lower Bound Derivation**
The **height** \(h\) of the decision tree (i.e., the worst-case number of comparisons) is constrained by the number of leaves in a binary tree:
\[
2^h \geq \text{Number of leaves} \geq n!.
\]
Solving for \(h\):
\[
h \geq \log_2(n!).
\]

**Stirling's Approximation** approximates \(n!\):
\[
n! \approx \sqrt{2\pi n} \left(\frac{n}{e}\right)^n.
\]
Taking the logarithm:
\[
\log_2(n!) \approx \log_2\left(\sqrt{2\pi n} \left(\frac{n}{e}\right)^n\right) = \frac{1}{2}\log_2(2\pi n) + n\log_2\left(\frac{n}{e}\right).
\]
Simplifying:
\[
\log_2(n!) = n\log_2 n - n\log_2 e + \frac{1}{2}\log_2(2\pi n).
\]
The dominant term is \(n\log_2 n\), so:
\[
\log_2(n!) = \Theta(n \log n).
\]
Thus, \(h \geq \Omega(n \log n)\), proving that any comparison-based sorting algorithm requires at least \(\Omega(n \log n)\) comparisons.
